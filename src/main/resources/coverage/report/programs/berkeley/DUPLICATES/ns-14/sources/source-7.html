90 /**
91  * Performs recovery when an Environment is opened.
92  *
93  * TODO: Need a description of the recovery algorithm here.  For some related
94  * information, see the Checkpointer class comments.
95  *
96  * Recovery, the INList and Eviction
97  * =================================
98  * There are two major steps in recovery: 1) recover the mapping database and
99  * the INs for all other databases, 2) recover the LNs for the other databases.
100  * In the buildTree method, step 1 comes before the call to buildINList and
101  * step 2 comes after that.  The INList is not maintained in step 1.
102  *
103  * The INList is not maintained in the step 1 because there is no benefit -- we
104  * cannot evict anyway as explained below -- and there are potential drawbacks
105  * to maintaining it: added complexity and decreased performance.  The
106  * drawbacks are described in more detail further below.
107  *
108  * Even if the INList were maintained in step 1, eviction could not be enabled
109  * until step 2, because logging is not allowed until all the INs are in place.
110  * In principle we could evict non-dirty nodes in step 1, but since recovery is
111  * dirtying the tree as it goes, there would be little or nothing that is
112  * non-dirty and could be evicted.
113  *
114  * Therefore, the INList has an 'enabled' mode that is initially false (in step
115  * 1) and is set to true by buildINList, just before step 2.  The mechanism for
116  * adding nodes to the INList is skipped when it is disabled.  In addition to
117  * enabling it, buildINList populates it from the contents of the Btrees that
118  * were constructed in step 1.  In step 2, eviction is invoked explicitly by
119  * calling EnvironmentImpl.invokeEvictor often during recovery.  This is
120  * important since the background evictor thread is not yet running.
121  *
122  * An externally visible limitation created by this situation is that the nodes
123  * placed in the Btree during step 1 must all fit in memory, since no eviction
124  * is performed.  So memory is a limiting factor in how large a recovery can be
125  * performed.  Since eviction is allowed in step 2, and step 2 is where the
126  * bulk of the recovery is normally performed, this limitation of step 1 hasn't
127  * been a critical problem.
128  *
129  * Maintaining the INList
130  * ----------------------
131  * In this section we consider the impact of maintaining the INList in step 1,
132  * if this were done in a future release.  It is being considered for a future
133  * release so we can rely on the INList to reference INs by node ID in the
134  * in-memory representation of an IN (see the Big Memory SR [#22292]).
135  *
136  * To maintain the INList in step 1, when a branch of a tree (a parent IN) is
137  * spliced in, the previous branch (all of the previous node's children) would
138  * have to be removed from the INList.  Doing this incorrectly could cause an
139  * OOME, and it may also have a performance impact.
140  *
141  * The performance impact of removing the previous branch from the INList is
142  * difficult to estimate.  In the normal case (recovery after normal shutdown),
143  * very few nodes will be replaced because normally only nodes at the max flush
144  * level are replayed, and the slots they are placed into will be empty (not
145  * resident).  Here is description of a worst case scenario, which is when
146  * there is a crash near the end of a checkpoint:
147  *
148  *  + The last checkpoint is large, includes all nodes in the tree, is mostly
149  *    complete, but was not finished (no CkptEnd).  The middle INs (above BIN
150  *    and below the max flush level) must be replayed (see Checkpointer and
151  *    Provisional.BEFORE_CKPT_END).
152  *
153  *  + For these middle INs, the INs at each level are placed in the tree and
154  *    replace any IN present in the slot.  For the bottom-most level of middle
155  *    INs (level 2), these don't replace a node (the slot will be empty because
156  *    BINs are never replayed).  But for the middle INs in all levels above
157  *    that, they replace a node that was fetched earlier; it was fetched
158  *    because it is the parent of a node at a lower level that was replayed.
159  *
160  *  + In the worst case, all INs from level 3 to R-1, where R is the root
161  *    level, would be replayed and replace a node.  However, it seems the
162  *    replaced node would not have resident children in the scenario described,
163  *    so the cost of removing it from the INList does not seem excessive.
164  *
165  *  + Here's an example illustrating this scenario.  The BINs and their parents
166  *    (as a sub-tree) are logged first, followed by all dirty INs at the next
167  *    level, etc.
168  *
169  *    0050 CkptStart
170  *    0100 BIN level 1
171  *    0200 BIN level 1
172  *    ...
173  *    1000 IN level 2, parent of 0100, 0200, etc.
174  *    1100 BIN level 1
175  *    1200 BIN level 1
176  *    ...
177  *    2000 IN level 2, parent of 1100, 1200, etc.
178  *    ...
179  *    7000 IN level 2, last level 2 IN logged
180  *    8000 IN level 3, parent of 1000, 2000, etc.
181  *    ...
182  *    9000 IN level 4, parent of 8000, etc.
183  *    ...
184  *    
185  *                  9000       level 4
186  *                 /
187  *            ----8000----     level 3
188  *           /  /         \
189  *       1000  2000    ......  level 2
190  *
191  *                             BINs not shown
192  *
193  *    Only the root (if it happens to be logged right before the crash) is
194  *    non-provisional.  We'll assume in this example that the root was not
195  *    logged.  Level 2 through R-1 are logged as Provisional.BEFORE_CKPT_END,
196  *    and treated as non-provisional (replayed) by recovery because there is no
197  *    CkptEnd.
198  *
199  *    When 1000 (and all other nodes at level 2) is replayed, it is placed into
200  *    an empty slot.
201  *
202  *    When 8000 (and all other INs at level 3 and higher, below the root) is
203  *    replayed, it will replace a resident node that was fetched and placed in
204  *    the slot when replaying its children.  The replaced node is one not
205  *    shown, and assumed to have been logged sometime prior to this checkpoint.
206  *    The replaced node will have all the level 2 nodes that were replayed
207  *    earlier (1000, 2000, etc.) as its resident children, and these are the
208  *    nodes that would have to be removed from the INList, if recovery were
209  *    changed to place INs on the INList in step 1.
210  *
211  *    So if the INs were placed on the INList, in this worst case scenario, all
212  *    INs from level 3 to R-1 will be replayed, and all their immediate
213  *    children would need to be removed from the INList.  Grandchildren would
214  *    not be resident.  In other words, all nodes at level 2 and above (except
215  *    the root) would be removed from the INList and replaced by a node being
216  *    replayed.
217  *
218  * When there is a normal shutdown, we don't know of scenarios that would cause
219  * this sort of INList thrashing.  So perhaps maintaining the INList in step 1
220  * could be justified, if the additional recovery cost after a crash is
221  * acceptable.
222  *
223  * Or, a potential solution for the worst case scenario above might be to place
224  * the resident child nodes in the new parent, rather than discarding them and
225  * removing them from the INList.  This would have the benefit of populating
226  * the cache and not wasting the work done to read and replay these nodes.
227  * OTOH, it may cause OOME if too much of the tree is loaded in step 1.
228  */

569         }

572          * The last valid LSN should point to the start of the last valid log
573          * entry, while the end of the log should point to the first byte of
574          * blank space, so these two should not be the same.
575          */
576         assert (reader.getLastValidLsn() != reader.getEndOfLog()):
577         "lastUsed=" + DbLsn.getNoFormatString(reader.getLastValidLsn()) +
578             " end=" + DbLsn.getNoFormatString(reader.getEndOfLog());

581         if (!readOnly) {
582             reader.setEndOfFile();
583         }

586         info.lastUsedLsn = reader.getLastValidLsn();
587         info.nextAvailableLsn = reader.getEndOfLog();

595     /**
596      * Find the last checkpoint and establish the firstActiveLsn point,
597      * checkpoint start, and checkpoint end.
598      */
599     private void findLastCheckpoint()
600         throws IOException, DatabaseException {
601 
602         startupTracker.start(Phase.FIND_LAST_CKPT);
603         startupTracker.setProgress(RecoveryProgress.FIND_LAST_CKPT);
604         Counter counter = startupTracker.getCounter(Phase.FIND_LAST_CKPT);
605 
606         /*
607          * The checkpointLsn might have been already found when establishing
608          * the end of the log.  If it was found, then partialCheckpointStartLsn
609          * was also found.  If it was not found, search backwards for it now
610          * and also set partialCheckpointStartLsn.
611          */
612         if (info.checkpointEndLsn == DbLsn.NULL_LSN) {
613 
614             /*
615              * Search backwards though the log for a checkpoint end entry and a
616              * root entry.
617              */
618             CheckpointFileReader searcher =
619                 new CheckpointFileReader(envImpl, readBufferSize, false,
620                                          info.lastUsedLsn, DbLsn.NULL_LSN,
621                                          info.nextAvailableLsn);
622 
623             while (searcher.readNextEntry()) {
624                 counter.incNumRead();
625                 counter.incNumProcessed();
626 
627                 /*
628                  * Continue iterating until we find a checkpoint end entry.
629                  * While we're at it, remember the last root seen in case we
630                  * don't find a checkpoint end entry.
631                  */
632                 if (searcher.isCheckpointEnd()) {
633 
634                     /*
635                      * We're done, the checkpoint end will tell us where the
636                      * root is.
637                      */
638                     info.checkpointEndLsn = searcher.getLastLsn();
639                     break;
640                 } else if (searcher.isCheckpointStart()) {
641 
642                     /*
643                      * Remember the first CkptStart following the CkptEnd.
644                      */
645                     info.partialCheckpointStartLsn = searcher.getLastLsn();
646 
647                 } else if (searcher.isDbTree()) {
648 
649                     /*
650                      * Save the last root that was found in the log in case we
651                      * don't see a checkpoint.
652                      */
653                     if (info.useRootLsn == DbLsn.NULL_LSN) {
654                         info.useRootLsn = searcher.getLastLsn();
655                     }
656                 }
657             }
658             counter.setRepeatIteratorReads(searcher.getNRepeatIteratorReads());
659         }
660 
661         /*
662          * If we haven't found a checkpoint, we'll have to recover without
663          * one. At a minimium, we must have found a root.
664          */
665         if (info.checkpointEndLsn == DbLsn.NULL_LSN) {
666             info.checkpointStartLsn = DbLsn.NULL_LSN;
667             info.firstActiveLsn = DbLsn.NULL_LSN;
668         } else {
669             /* Read in the checkpoint entry. */
670             CheckpointEnd checkpointEnd = (CheckpointEnd)
671                 (envImpl.getLogManager().getEntry(info.checkpointEndLsn));
672             info.checkpointEnd = checkpointEnd;
673             info.checkpointStartLsn = checkpointEnd.getCheckpointStartLsn();
674             info.firstActiveLsn = checkpointEnd.getFirstActiveLsn();
675 
676             /*
677              * Use the last checkpoint root only if there is no later root.
678              * The latest root has the correct per-DB utilization info.
679              */
680             if (checkpointEnd.getRootLsn() != DbLsn.NULL_LSN &&
681                 info.useRootLsn == DbLsn.NULL_LSN) {
682                 info.useRootLsn = checkpointEnd.getRootLsn();
683             }
684 
685             /* Init the checkpointer's id sequence.*/
686             envImpl.getCheckpointer().setCheckpointId(checkpointEnd.getId());
687         }
688 
689         /*
690          * Let the rollback tracker know where the checkpoint start is.
691          * Rollback periods before the checkpoint start do not need to be
692          * processed.
693          */
694         rollbackTracker.setCheckpointStart(info.checkpointStartLsn);
695 
696         startupTracker.stop(Phase.FIND_LAST_CKPT);
697 
698         if (info.useRootLsn == DbLsn.NULL_LSN) {
699             throw new EnvironmentFailureException
700                 (envImpl,
701                  EnvironmentFailureReason.LOG_INTEGRITY,
702                  "This environment's log file has no root. Since the root " +
703                  "is the first entry written into a log at environment " +
704                  "creation, this should only happen if the initial creation " +
705                  "of the environment was never checkpointed or synced. " +
706                  "Please move aside the existing log files to allow the " +
707                  "creation of a new environment");
708         }
709     }

841

863     /**
864      * Perform two passes for the INs of a given level. Root INs must be
865      * processed first to account for splits/compressions that were done
866      * during/after a checkpoint [#14424] [#24663].
867      *
868      * Splits and compression require logging up to the root of the tree, to
869      * ensure that all INs are properly returned to the correct position at
870      * recovery. In other words, splits and compression ensure that the
871      * creation and deletion of all nodes is promptly logged.
872      *
873      * However, checkpoints are not propagated to the top of the tree, in
874      * order to conserve on logging. Because of that, a great-aunt situation
875      * can occur, where an ancestor of a given node can be logged without
876      * referring to the latest on-disk position of the node, because that
877      * ancestor was part of a split or compression.
878      *
879      * Take this scenario:
880      *     Root-A
881      *      /    \
882      *    IN-B   IN-C
883      *   /      / | \
884      *  BIN-D
885      *  /
886      * LN-E
887      *
888      * 1) LN-E  is logged, BIN-D is dirtied
889      * 2) BIN-D is logged during a checkpoint, IN-B is dirtied
890      * 3) IN-C  is split and Root-A is logged
891      * 4) We recover using Root-A and the BIN-D logged at (2) is lost
892      *
893      * At (3) when Root-A is logged, it points to an IN-B on disk that does not
894      * point to the most recent BIN-D
895      *
896      * At (4) when we recover, although we will process the BIN-D logged at (2)
897      * and splice it into the tree, the Root-A logged at (3) is processed last
898      * and overrides the entire subtree containing BIN-D
899      *
900      * This could be addressed by always logging to the root at every
901      * checkpoint. Barring that, we address it by replaying the root INs first,
902      * and then all non-root INs.
903      *
904      * It is important that no IN is replayed that would cause a fetch of an
905      * older IN version which has been replaced by a newer version in the
906      * checkpoint interval. If the newer version were logged as the result of
907      * log cleaning, and we attempt to fetch the older version, this would
908      * cause a LOG_FILE_NOT_FOUND exception. The replay of the root INs in the
909      * first pass is safe, because it won't cause a fetch [#24663]. The replay
910      * of INs in the second pass is safe because only nodes at maxFlushLevel
911      * were logged non-provisionally and only these nodes are replayed.
912      *
913      * @param mappingTree if true, we're building the mapping tree
914      */
915     private void buildINs(
916         boolean mappingTree, 
917         Phase phaseA,
918         Phase phaseB,
919         RecoveryProgress progressA,
920         RecoveryProgress progressB)
921         throws DatabaseException {
922 
923         /*
924          * Pass a: Replay root INs.
925          */
926         startupTracker.start(phaseA);
927         startupTracker.setProgress(progressA);
928 
929         if (mappingTree) {
930             readRootINsAndTrackIds(startupTracker.getCounter(phaseA));
931         } else {
932             readRootINs(startupTracker.getCounter(phaseA));
933         }
934         startupTracker.stop(phaseA);
935 
936         /*
937          * Pass b: Replay non-root INs.
938          */
939         startupTracker.start(phaseB);
940         startupTracker.setProgress(progressB);
941 
942         readNonRootINs(mappingTree, startupTracker.getCounter(phaseB));
943 
944         startupTracker.stop(phaseB);
945     }

2734      * no checkpoint

2738      * anything as obsolete because the log and tree LSNs are equal.  100 is
2739      * never counted obsolete because it is not in the recovery interval.

2742      * is not found in the tree because it was compressed and the IN was
2743      * flushed non-provisionally.

2761          * during normal processing and it should be counted here only if its
2762          * obsoleteness was not made durable before the crash, i.e., if it is
2763          * after the latest FSLN for the containing log file. No need to record
2764          * L's LSN in the tracker, because the cleaner already knows that all
2765          * immediately-obsolete logrecs are obsolete.
2766          */

2768             tracker.countObsoleteIfUncounted(

2770                  false /*trackOffset*/);
2771         }

2775          * or the abortLsn are before the ckpt start, then they are already
2776          * counted as obsolete because utilization info is flushed just before
2777          * ckpt end. And if they are after the ckpt start, then they have been
2778          * processed earlier in this RedoLNs pass and as a result counted by
2779          * the countObsoleteIfUncounted() call above.
2780          */

2786         if (treeLsn != DbLsn.NULL_LSN) {

2788             final int cmpLogLsnToTreeLsn = DbLsn.compareTo(logrecLsn, treeLsn);
2789 
2790             if (cmpLogLsnToTreeLsn < 0) {
2791 
2792                 /*
2793                  * L < TL.
2794                  *
2795                  * In normal standalone recovery, if L < TL, we can assume
2796                  * that TL belongs to a committed txn. This is because the
2797                  * undo phase happened first, and any uncommitted lsns would
2798                  * have been removed from the tree. But for replicated and
2799                  * prepared txns, this assumption is not true; such txns
2800                  * may be committed or aborted later on. So, TL may belong to
2801                  * a later, uncommitted txn. [#17710] [#17022]
2802                  *
2803                  * L may be obsolete. It is obsolete iff:
2804                  *
2805                  * 1. it is immediately obsolete, or
2806                  * 2. TT is committed (we can check this by checking whether
2807                  *    TL is not among the resurrected LSNs), or
2808                  * 3. L is not the last logrec on R by T.
2809                  *
2810                  * L is not obsolete if TT is not committed and L is the last
2811                  * logrec on R by T. These conditions, together with the fact
2812                  * that L < TL imply that T != TT and L is the abortLsn of TT.
2813                  *
2814                  * We have already handled case 1 above. We cannot check for
2815                  * case 3, so we will conservatively assume L is indeed
2816                  * obsolete but record its LSN in the tracker only if we know
2817                  * for sure that it is obsolete, ie, if TT is committed.
2818                  *
2819                  * Notice also that if L is indeed obsolete, we cannot be
2820                  * sure which logrec caused L to become obsolete during
2821                  * normal processing. So, here we conservatively assume
2822                  * that it was TL that made L obsolete, and pass TL as the
2823                  * "lsn" param to countObsoleteIfUncounted(). This will
2824                  * result in double counting if (a) another logrec L',
2825                  * with L < L' < TL, made L obsolete, an FSLN for L's
2826                  * logfile was logged after L' and before TL, and no other
2827                  * FSLN for the that logfile was logged after TL. 
2828                  */

2830                     tracker.countObsoleteIfUncounted(
2831                         logrecLsn, treeLsn, null,
2832                         fetchLNSize(db, logrecSize, logrecLsn), db.getId(),
2833                         !resurrectedLsns.contains(treeLsn) /*trackOffset*/);
2834                 }
2835

2842                  * here as well, with the roles of L and TL reversed.
2843                  *
2844                  * Notice that in this case, there cannot be another R logrec
2845                  * L' such that TL < L' < L. To see why, assume L' exists and
2846                  * consider these 2 cases: (a) L' > ckpt_start. Then L' was
2847                  * replayed earlier during the current RedoLNs pass, so at this
2848                  * time, TL must be L'. (b)  L' < ckpt_start. Then L' <= TL,
2849                  * because the ckpt writes all dirty BINs to the log and
2850                  * RedoINs is done before RedoLNs.
2851                  *
2852                  * The above observation implies that either T == TT or TL is
2853                  * the abortLSN of L. If TL == AL, then it is T's commit logrec
2854                  * that made TL obsolete during normal processing. However, here
2855                  * we pass L as the lsn param of countObsoleteIfUncounted().
2856                  * As explained below this can result in missing counting for
2857                  * a real obsolete logrec.

2860                  * for the same reason described above in the case of

2869                 }
2870             }
2871         }
2872 
2873         /*

2875          * the commit logrec of T that makes abortLSN obsolete. So, we pass
2876          * commitLSN as the "lsn" param to the countObsoleteIfUncounted()
2877          * call below. However, to avoid excessive double-counting, we don't
2878          * always call countObsoleteIfUncounted(AL, T-cmt). In relatively
2879          * rare scenarios, this can result in failing to count AL as obsolete.
2880          * Consider the following cases:
2881          *
2882          * TL < L
2883          * -------
2884          *
2885          * If TL < L we don't call countObsoleteIfUncounted() on AL, because
2886          * in most cases this has been done already during the current RedoLNs
2887          * pass or AL was counted durably as obsolete during normal processing.
2888          * The reasoning is as follows. As explained above, if TL < L, one of
2889          * the following is true:
2890          *
2891          * (a) TL == AL.
2892          *
2893          *     TL/AL --- TT-cmt --- L --- (FSLN)? --- T-cmt
2894          *
2895          *     countObsoleteIfUncounted(TL, L) was called above. If FSLN
2896          *     exists, this call did not count TL. However, FSLN does not
2897          *     contain TL, because it is T-cmt that recorded TL as obsolete.
2898          *     Therefore, assuming no other FSLN exists after T-cmt, we miss
2899          *     counting TL by not calling countObsoleteIfUncounted(TL, T-cmt).
2900          *
2901          *     However, in most cases, there won't be any FSLN between L and
2902          *     T-cmt, or there will be another FLSN after T-cmt. As a result,
2903          *     the countObsoleteIfUncounted(TL, L) call suffices. Therefore,
2904          *     we prefer to occasionally miss an abortLSN than doing too much
2905          *     double counting.
2906          *
2907          * (b1) T == TT and TL < ckpt_start. In
2908          *
2909          *      AL --- TL --- ckpt-start --- L --- T-cmt --- (FSLN)?
2910          *
2911          *      TL and L have the same abortLSN and the same commitLSN. TL is
2912          *      not processed during this RedoLNs pass, so unless an FSLN was
2913          *      logged during normal processing after T-cmt, we miss counting
2914          *      AL. Notice that an FSLN will exist if T-cmt occurs before
2915          *      ckpt-end, which is the more common case.
2916          *
2917          * (b2) T == TT and TL > ckpt_start
2918          *
2919          *      ckpt-start --- TL --- L --- (FSLN-1)? --- T-cmt --- (FSLN)?
2920          *
2921          *      TL and L have the same abortLSN and the same commitLSN.
2922          *      Furthermore, TL was processed during the current RedoLNs pass.
2923          *      We assume that what ever was done about AL during the
2924          *      processing of TL was correct and we don't repeat it.
2925          *
2926          * L < TL
2927          * -------
2928          *
2929          * (a) ckpt_start --- AL --- L --- T-cmt --- (FSLN)? --- TL --- (FSLN)?
2930          *
2931          *     AL was processed earlier in this RedoLNs pass. When it was
2932          *     processed, it was < TL, so countObsoleteIfUncounted(AL, TL)
2933          *     was called. There is no reason to count again.
2934          *
2935          * (b) ckpt_start --- AL --- L --- TL --- (FSLN))? --- T-cmt --- (FSLN)?
2936          *
2937          *     AL was processed earlier in this RedoLNs pass. When it was
2938          *     processed, it was < TL, so countObsoleteIfUncounted(AL, TL)
2939          *     was called. To avoid double counting, we will not call
2940          *     countObsoleteIfUncounted(AL, t-cmt). However, in this case
2941          *     we will fail counting AL as obsolete if there is an FSLN
2942          *     between TL and T-cmt and no FSLN after T-cmt.
2943          *
2944          * (c) AL --- ckpt_start --- L --- TL
2945          *
2946          *     In this case we call countObsoleteIfUncounted(AL, t-cmt)
2947          *
2948          * L == TL
2949          * -------
2950          *
2951          * (a) ckpt_start --- AL --- L/TL --- (FSLN)? --- T-cmt --- (FSLN)?
2952          *
2953          *     Same as L < TL, case (b).
2954          *
2955          * (c) AL --- ckpt_start --- L --- TL
2956          *
